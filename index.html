What is Fast Fashion?

Fast fashion refers to clothing designs that are quickly produced and made available in stores to meet current trends. These collections are often inspired by styles presented at Fashion Week or worn by celebrities. Fast fashion enables the general public to buy the latest trendy clothing at a reasonable cost. Shopping for clothing used to be a special occasion, and people would save up to purchase new clothes at specific times of the year. Fashion enthusiasts would get a sneak peek of upcoming styles through fashion shows that displayed new collections and clothing lines several months before they were available in stores. However, this began to change in the late 1990s when shopping started to be seen as a form of entertainment, and there was an increase in spending on clothing. This is when fast fashion emerged â€” cheap and trendy clothes that are mass-produced at low cost, allowing consumers to feel like they are wearing the latest styles seen on the runway or worn by celebrities. The aim of fast fashion is to quickly produce affordable clothing to satisfy consumer demand. The concept is that consumers want high fashion at a low cost, and because the quality of the garments is not significant, they are not meant to be worn for multiple times or years.

Environmental Concerns

The environmental consequences of fast fashion include the depletion of non-renewable resources, the release of greenhouse gases, and the extensive use of water and energy, needing approximately 700 gallons to produce one cotton shirt and 2000 gallons to make a pair of jeans. Textile dyeing is also a major source of water pollution, as the leftover water from the dyeing process is often discharged into ditches, streams, or rivers, making it the world's second-largest polluter of water. Additionally, brands utilize synthetic fibers such as polyester, nylon, and acrylic, which can take hundreds of years to biodegrade. A 2017 report from the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) estimated that 35% of all microplastics in the ocean result from washing synthetic textiles like polyester. The 2015 documentary, The True Cost, revealed that the world now consumes approximately 80 billion new pieces of clothing annually, which is a 400% increase from twenty years ago. The average American now generates 82 pounds of textile each year. Furthermore, the tanning process is one of the most environmentally harmful stages in the fashion supply chain due to the non-biodegradable chemicals used, such as mineral salts, coal-tar derivatives, and various oils and dyes, which contaminate water sources. Moreover, the process of turning plastic fibers into textiles is highly energy-intensive and relies on large quantities of petroleum, leading to the release of acids such as hydrogen chloride. The production of cotton, which is used in many fast fashion items, is also environmentally unfriendly, as the pesticides for its growth pose health risks to farmers.

Harms to Humans

The fast fashion industry not only introduces harmful chemicals to the environment but also poses health risks to workers and consumers. Pesticides used in manufacturing can lead to acute and chronic health issues for workers, while consumers are exposed to harmful chemicals in garments. Additionally, the export of second-hand clothing to low-income countries can negatively impact local economies and traditional industries. The desire for low-cost garments also perpetuates low wages and poor working conditions in the countries where factories are located. Tragic events, such as the Rana Plaza building collapse in Bangladesh, have shed light on the bad conditions in which fast fashion industries operate. Textile workers are exposed to various occupational hazards, including respiratory and musculoskeletal harms. Reported health conditions include endocrine damage, reproductive harms, accident injuries, and death. These effects are spread disproportionately across genders, as most workers in these factories are young women. An estimated 80% of global workers in the garment industry are women, and discrimination, gender pay gaps, and sexual harassment continue to be reported. While many companies have systems to remedy this, inequalities continue to exist in many of these garment manufacturing environments. A reported 9 out of 10 garment workers in Bangladesh are paid so unfairly for their labor that they cannot afford food for themselves or their families. However, to provide workers with a livable wage would cost some companies as little as an estimated 1% of the retail price of garments. The gross injustices occurring within the fast fashion industry stand against the narrative that fast fashion benefits low-income people. Rather, it exploits workers and consumers alike.

FAQ

It is estimated that only 2% of fashion workers worldwide are paid a livable salary. Fast fashion factories are often located in developing countries with cheap labor and limited worker's rights. The top garment exporting countries are China, Bangladesh, Vietnam, and India. Fast fashion retailers also employ workers in countries like Indonesia, Madagascar, Haiti, Turkey, and the Philippines. Beside the developing nations, some companies also use unethical practices in developed countries like the US and the UK, exploiting cheap labor. Using a pay per garment system, these companies loophole their way out of paying minimum wage. In the United States, the majority of these workers are immigrant women, many of whom are undocumented. A report from the U.S. Department of labor found that American garment workers are paid as little as 1.58 dollars per hour.

Fast fashion has contributed to the creation of some of the wealthiest individuals in the world. Thanks to the global success of Zara, the company's founder, Amancio Ortega, is currently the 6th richest person in the world, with a net worth of $55.1 billion in 2020. Meanwhile, garment workers continue to be among the poorest people on the planet. The CEO of one of the world's largest brands, including Zara's parent company Inditex and the Swedish fast fashion brand H&M, only need to work for four days to earn what a Bangladeshi garment worker earns in their entire lifetime.

There are a few options instead of fast fashion: wearing second-hand or vintage clothing, recycling our unwanted garments, buying less and shopping less, and making smart choices when shopping at sustainable fashion brands. It is crucial to understand that any "sustainable lines" from fast fashion are not actually sustainable. These marketing strategies are meant to enhance brands images.

The economic impact of fast fashion is easily disproven. The clothing industry has been growing by as much as 8% annually, with quick styles driving this growth. It is assessed to reach $39.84 billion by 2025. Quick fashion organizations employ thousands of people and generate millions in profits each year. However, critics argue that fast fashion has negative economic consequences. They claim that it costs countries and their economics when workers fail or become sick or injured due to poor working conditions, which are often associated with the fast fashion industry.

Unfortunately, many of the major players in the fashion industry are fast fashion brands. Many started as small European shops in the '70s and '80s, but with the advent of globalization in the '90s, these family-owned businesses rapidly expanded into multi-billion dollar international corporations. Zara, H&M, Forever 21, Uniqlo, Primark, Shein, and Fashion Nova are just a few of the fast fashion brands that should be avoided. It's worth noting that not all fast fashion brands are immediately recognizable as such, Urban Outfitters, American Eagle, Anthropologie, and Topshop are also part of this category.

Organic or recycled cotton, hemp, and organic linen are among the most sustainable fabrics. Organic cotton is free from harmful pesticides and is produced without the use of the hazardous chemicals used in conventional cotton production. Hemp is a highly durable fabric commonly used in clothing, and it has naturally insulating and cooling properties. Additionally, the process of spinning hemp into fabric requires no chemicals, making it environmentally friendly. Hemp fabrics also become softer with each wash making them a favorite for many. Linen, made from a plant, is lightweight and favored for summer clothing. Its manufacturing process requires minimal water, and untreated organic linen is highly sustainable.

There are a number of tricks that fashion stores use. The clothes are intentionally designed to feel slightly off-season, pressuring you to buy more. They advertise discount clothing to sell lower quality products of the same brand, making you spend more money. They also use shiny objects and surfaces to make their products seem better, taking advantage of our attraction to shiny, reflective things. Besides, the clothes are designed to be disposable and fall apart, making you buy more of the same cheaply-made garment.
